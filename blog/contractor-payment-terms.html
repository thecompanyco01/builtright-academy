Contractor Payment Terms: How to Get Paid on Time

Cash flow problems kill more contracting businesses than bad work or low prices. Here's how to structure your payment terms, collect deposits, handle late payers, and ensure you never fund a customer's project out of your own pocket again.

Here's a number that should scare every contractor: the average small contractor has $25,000–$50,000 in outstanding receivables at any given time. That's YOUR money sitting in someone else's bank account, funding their project while you scramble to cover payroll and material costs.

Getting paid isn't a customer service issue — it's a survival issue. And the solution isn't chasing payments after the fact. It's structuring your terms upfront so late payment becomes nearly impossible.

1. Why Payment Terms Matter More Than You Think

Cash Flow Is King

Profit and cash flow are not the same thing. You can have $50,000 in profitable jobs completed and still not be able to make payroll if those customers haven't paid yet. 82% of small businesses that fail cite cash flow problems as the primary cause — not lack of work, not poor quality, not competition.

You're Not a Bank

When you buy materials and pay labor upfront but don't collect payment until the job is done (or worse, 30 days later), you're essentially financing your customer's project. You're giving them an interest-free loan. Banks charge 8–20% interest for this service. You're doing it for free.

The math is brutal: A $15,000 bathroom remodel with materials purchased upfront ($4,500), two weeks of labor paid out ($3,600), and the customer paying "upon completion" means you've invested $8,100 of your own money for 2–3 weeks (or longer if there are delays). If you're doing this across 3–4 jobs simultaneously, you need $25,000–$35,000 in working capital just to function. Most small contractors don't have that cushion.

2. Payment Structures by Job Size

The right payment structure depends on job size and type. Here's what works:

Small Jobs: Under $1,000

Structure: Payment in full upon completion
Collect at: Before you leave the job site
Method: Credit card, check, or digital payment (Venmo, Zelle)
Rule: Never leave a job site without collecting payment on small jobs. Have a card reader in your truck. Send the invoice on-site and collect immediately.

Medium Jobs: $1,000–$5,000

Structure: 50% deposit upfront, 50% upon completion
Collect deposit: Before scheduling the work
Collect balance: On the day of completion, before leaving
Rule: Don't order materials until the deposit clears. The deposit should cover your material costs at minimum.

Large Jobs: $5,000–$25,000

Structure: 33/33/34 or 40/30/30
Example (33/33/34):
• 33% deposit before work begins
• 33% at project midpoint (defined milestone)
• 34% upon completion
Rule: Define milestones clearly in the contract. "Midpoint" means rough-in complete, not "halfway through the calendar." Tie payments to verifiable deliverables.

Major Projects: $25,000+

Structure: Progress payments tied to milestones
Example:
• 25% deposit upon signing
• 25% upon completion of rough-in/framing
• 25% upon completion of finish work
• 15% upon final inspection/completion
• 10% retention (released 30 days after completion)
Rule: Never have more money invested in a job than you've collected. At every stage, your collected payments should exceed your costs to date.

State laws matter: Some states limit how much contractors can collect as a deposit. For example, California limits deposits to $1,000 or 10% of the contract price (whichever is less) for home improvement contracts. Check your state's contractor licensing board for specific rules before setting your payment terms.

3. Getting the Deposit Right

Why Deposits Are Non-Negotiable

A deposit does three things:

  1. Commits the customer: People who put money down cancel far less often than those who just verbally agree
  2. Covers your upfront costs: Materials, permits, and scheduling are real expenses before work begins
  3. Filters tire-kickers: A customer who won't put down a deposit is a customer who'll be difficult to collect from later

How to Ask for the Deposit

Make it part of the process, not a negotiation:

"To get you on the schedule, we collect a 50% deposit which covers materials and permits. We accept credit card, check, or bank transfer. Which is easiest for you?"

Notice: you're not asking IF they'll pay a deposit. You're asking HOW. This is assumed close — the deposit is standard, not optional.

What If They Push Back on the Deposit?

  • "I've never had to pay a deposit before." Response: "It's industry standard for professional contractors. It covers material procurement and guarantees your spot on our schedule."
  • "Can I pay when the work is done?" Response: "Our payment terms are 50% to schedule, 50% on completion. This protects both of us and ensures we can secure materials at today's prices."
  • "I don't have the deposit right now." Response: "I understand. We can schedule your project for when the deposit is ready. We're currently booking [X weeks] out, so the sooner we get started, the sooner we can get you taken care of."

If a customer refuses any deposit, that's a major red flag. Walk away. A customer who won't commit $500 upfront is a customer who won't pay $5,000 later.

4. Invoicing Best Practices

Invoice Immediately

Send the invoice the moment the work is complete — ideally while you're still on-site. Every day you delay invoicing, your average collection time increases. Tools like Jobber, Housecall Pro, and QuickBooks let you create and send invoices from your phone in under 2 minutes.

What Every Invoice Should Include

  • Invoice number and date
  • Your company name, address, and contact info
  • Customer name and project address
  • Detailed line items matching the original estimate
  • Payment terms (Due on Receipt, Net 15, etc.)
  • Amount due and amount already paid (deposit)
  • Accepted payment methods
  • Late payment policy
  • A "Pay Now" link if using digital invoicing

Net Terms Explained

  • Due on Receipt: Payment expected immediately. Best for residential service calls.
  • Net 15: Payment due within 15 days. Good for residential projects.
  • Net 30: Payment due within 30 days. Standard for commercial work and GC-to-sub relationships.
  • Net 45/60/90: Avoid these if possible. They're common in commercial construction but murder your cash flow.

Best practice for residential: Use "Due on Receipt" or "Due Upon Completion" for all residential work. Net 30 is for commercial and B2B relationships only. Homeowners have no reason to need 30 days to pay — they have credit cards and bank accounts.

5. Handling Late Payments

Late Fee Policy

Include a late fee clause in every contract and invoice. Standard late fees:

  • Flat fee: $25–$50 for invoices under $1,000; $50–$100 for invoices over $1,000
  • Percentage: 1.5–2% per month on the outstanding balance (18–24% annualized)
  • Both: $50 flat fee + 1.5% per month after 30 days

Important: Check your state's usury laws for maximum allowable interest rates on overdue accounts. Most states cap between 12–25% annually.

The Collection Escalation Process

Day 1 (Due Date)

Send automated payment reminder: "Your invoice #XXX for $X,XXX is due today. Pay securely online: [link]"

Day 3 (3 Days Late)

Text or call: "Hi [Name], just a friendly reminder that your invoice for $X,XXX is due. Would you like me to resend the payment link?"

Day 7 (1 Week Late)

Phone call: Direct conversation about the overdue balance. Be professional but clear: "I need to get this resolved this week."

Day 14 (2 Weeks Late)

Formal letter (email and mail): "Your account is 14 days past due. A late fee of $XX has been applied. Payment is required within 7 days to avoid further action."

Day 30 (1 Month Late)

Final demand letter: "This is a final notice. Your account balance of $X,XXX is 30 days overdue. If payment is not received within 10 days, we will pursue collection through [mechanics lien / small claims court / collection agency]."

Day 45+ (Escalation)

File a mechanics lien (if applicable in your state), send to a collection agency, or file in small claims court. Don't bluff on this — if you say you'll take action, take action.

6. Legal Protections Every Contractor Needs

Written Contracts (Always)

Never start work without a signed contract, even for small jobs. The contract should include:

  • Complete scope of work
  • Total price and payment schedule
  • Timeline and milestones
  • Change order process (how changes affect price and schedule)
  • Late payment terms and interest
  • Dispute resolution (mediation before litigation)
  • Right to stop work for non-payment

Mechanics Liens

A mechanics lien is the most powerful collection tool available to contractors. It places a legal claim on the customer's property for the unpaid amount. The property can't be sold or refinanced until the lien is resolved.

  • Requirements vary by state — deadlines, notice requirements, and filing procedures differ
  • In most states, you must send a preliminary notice at the start of the project to preserve lien rights
  • Lien deadlines are strict (30–120 days from completion, depending on state)
  • Consult a construction attorney or use a service like LevelSet to manage lien rights

Stop Work Clauses

Your contract should include a clause allowing you to stop work if a progress payment is missed: "Contractor reserves the right to suspend work if any scheduled payment is not received within 7 days of the due date. Work will resume upon receipt of payment plus any applicable late fees."

7. Payment Methods That Get You Paid Faster

Accept Credit Cards (Non-Negotiable)

Yes, you'll pay 2.5–3.5% in processing fees. But consider:

  • Customers who pay by card pay immediately — no waiting for checks
  • Average ticket increases 15–20% when credit card is an option (customers buy upgrades)
  • You can process payment on-site before leaving
  • The processing fee is a business expense (tax deductible)

The 3% fee on a $2,000 job is $60. Getting paid today instead of chasing a check for 30 days is worth far more than $60.

Digital Payment Options

  • Square: 2.6% + $0.10 per tap/swipe. Free card reader. Simple setup.
  • Stripe (via invoicing software): 2.9% + $0.30. Best for online invoice payments.
  • Zelle: Free bank-to-bank transfer. Instant for many banks. No dispute/chargeback protection.
  • ACH bank transfer: $0.25–$1.00 per transaction. Best for large payments. Takes 2–3 business days.

Customer Financing

For big-ticket jobs ($5,000+), offering financing through Wisetack, GreenSky, or Hearth increases your close rate by 15–30%. The customer pays over time; you get paid in full within days. The financing fee (typically 3–8% of the project) is worth it for the conversion increase and immediate payment.

8. Special Considerations for Commercial Work

GC-to-Sub Payment Terms

If you're a subcontractor on commercial projects, payment terms are typically more aggressive against you:

  • Pay-when-paid clauses: The GC pays you when the owner pays them. This can delay payment 60–90+ days.
  • Retention: 5–10% of each payment is held until project completion. On a $50,000 sub contract, that's $2,500–$5,000 tied up for months.
  • Net 30/60/90: Common in commercial. Net 30 from invoice approval, not from invoice submission — approval can take 2–3 weeks itself.

Protecting Yourself on Commercial Jobs

  • Negotiate terms before signing: Push for Net 30 instead of Net 60. Negotiate lower retention (5% instead of 10%).
  • File preliminary notices: Protect your mechanics lien rights from day one
  • Track payment timelines: If a GC is consistently late, stop taking their work
  • Factor receivables if needed: Invoice factoring companies advance 80–90% of your invoice immediately for a 2–5% fee. Expensive, but keeps cash flowing on large commercial jobs.

The Bottom Line

Getting paid on time isn't about luck or having "good customers." It's about systems:

  1. Structure payments so you're never financing the customer's project
  2. Collect deposits before ordering materials or scheduling work
  3. Invoice immediately upon completion — not next week, today
  4. Accept credit cards so customers can pay on the spot
  5. Follow up systematically on overdue invoices
  6. Protect yourself legally with written contracts, lien rights, and stop-work clauses

Every dollar you collect faster is a dollar you can reinvest in your business. Stop being your customers' bank. Get paid for your work.
