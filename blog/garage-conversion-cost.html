Garage Conversion Cost in 2026 — Complete Price Guide by Project Type

Your garage is the easiest space in your home to convert into usable living area — no foundation to pour, no roof to build. But costs vary wildly depending on what you're converting it into. Here's what every project type actually costs, broken down component by component.

Garage conversions are one of the highest-ROI remodeling projects you can do — you're starting with an enclosed, roofed structure that already has a foundation and (usually) electrical. You're not building from scratch. You're upgrading.

But "garage conversion" covers everything from a basic insulation-and-drywall bedroom ($10,000) to a full accessory dwelling unit with kitchen, bathroom, and separate entrance ($80,000+). The project type determines the price, not the square footage.

I've seen homeowners get bids ranging from $15,000 to $90,000 for the same 400 sq ft garage — the difference was entirely about what they wanted inside it.

1. Garage Conversion Costs at a Glance

Cost by Conversion Type (Standard 2-Car Garage, ~400 Sq Ft)

  • Home office (no plumbing): $8,000–$18,000
  • Bedroom (no bathroom): $10,000–$22,000
  • Bedroom + bathroom: $18,000–$38,000
  • Home gym: $8,000–$20,000
  • Studio / workshop: $10,000–$25,000
  • ADU / apartment (full kitchen + bath): $40,000–$100,000
  • In-law suite (bedroom + bath + kitchenette): $30,000–$65,000

The cost per square foot for garage conversions ranges from $20–$250/sq ft depending on complexity. A basic conversion without plumbing averages $25–$50/sq ft. A full ADU runs $100–$250/sq ft — approaching new construction costs because you're essentially building a complete living unit.

2. Bedroom Conversion

Converting a garage to a bedroom is one of the most popular projects. It's relatively straightforward — you need insulation, drywall, flooring, electrical, HVAC, and an egress window. No plumbing unless you add a bathroom.

Bedroom Conversion Cost Breakdown (400 Sq Ft)

  • Garage door removal + wall framing: $1,500–$3,500
  • Insulation (walls + ceiling): $1,200–$3,000
  • Drywall (hang, tape, finish): $1,500–$3,500
  • Flooring (LVP or carpet): $1,200–$3,200
  • Electrical (outlets, lighting, circuits): $1,000–$2,500
  • HVAC extension (ductwork or mini-split): $1,500–$4,000
  • Egress window: $800–$2,500
  • Closet framing & shelving: $500–$1,500
  • Paint: $400–$800
  • Permits: $500–$1,500
  • Total: $10,100–$26,000

Egress requirement: Building code requires bedrooms to have an egress window — minimum 5.7 sq ft opening, sill no higher than 44 inches from the floor. Most garages don't have windows that meet this standard. Budget $800–$2,500 for cutting a new window opening, framing, and installing an egress-compliant window. This is non-negotiable — no permit will be issued without it.

Adding a Bathroom to a Bedroom Conversion

A bathroom turns a bedroom conversion into a true guest suite or in-law room. Expect to add $8,000–$18,000 for a full bathroom (toilet, sink, shower) depending on how far you are from existing plumbing lines.

  • Plumbing rough-in (drain, supply, vent): $3,000–$7,000
  • Bathroom fixtures (toilet, vanity, shower): $1,500–$4,000
  • Tile work (shower + floor): $2,000–$5,000
  • Exhaust fan, electrical, lighting: $500–$1,500
  • Concrete cutting (for slab drain): $1,000–$3,000

If the garage shares a wall with an existing bathroom or kitchen, plumbing costs drop significantly because you can tie into existing drain and supply lines. If the garage is on the opposite end of the house, the plumber has to run new lines — and if it's a slab foundation, that means cutting concrete.

3. Home Office Conversion

Home offices are the simplest and cheapest conversion. No plumbing, no egress window requirement (since it's not a bedroom), and fewer code considerations.

Home Office Conversion Cost Breakdown (400 Sq Ft)

  • Garage door removal + wall framing: $1,500–$3,500
  • Insulation (walls + ceiling): $1,200–$3,000
  • Drywall: $1,500–$3,500
  • Flooring (LVP or laminate): $1,200–$3,000
  • Electrical (20-amp circuits, data drops, outlets): $1,200–$3,000
  • HVAC (mini-split recommended): $1,500–$4,000
  • Window (natural light): $500–$1,500
  • Paint: $400–$800
  • Permits: $500–$1,200
  • Total: $9,500–$23,500

For a home office, invest more in electrical and data. Run at least two dedicated 20-amp circuits (separate from the house), install ethernet drops if you work from home on video calls, and consider acoustic insulation (Rockwool) if the garage shares a wall with a noisy room. The difference between a garage that "works as an office" and one that feels professional is about $2,000–$4,000 in thoughtful upgrades.

4. ADU / Apartment Conversion

This is the big one. An accessory dwelling unit (ADU) is a complete, self-contained living unit — kitchen, bathroom, sleeping area, and separate entrance. It's essentially building a small apartment inside your garage.

ADU Garage Conversion Cost Breakdown (400 Sq Ft)

  • Garage door removal + wall framing + exterior finish: $3,000–$6,000
  • Insulation (R-15 walls, R-38 ceiling): $2,000–$4,000
  • Drywall: $2,000–$4,000
  • Flooring (LVP throughout): $1,600–$3,600
  • Kitchen (cabinets, countertop, sink, appliances): $8,000–$20,000
  • Bathroom (full bath with shower): $6,000–$15,000
  • Plumbing (rough-in for kitchen + bath): $5,000–$12,000
  • Electrical (sub-panel, all circuits, lighting): $3,000–$8,000
  • HVAC (ductless mini-split): $3,000–$6,000
  • Separate electrical meter (if required): $1,000–$3,000
  • Separate entrance + exterior work: $1,500–$4,000
  • Fire separation (5/8" Type X drywall): $800–$2,000
  • Windows (2–3 egress-compliant): $1,500–$4,500
  • Paint, trim, finishing: $1,000–$2,500
  • Permits & plan review: $1,500–$5,000
  • Architectural/engineering plans: $2,000–$6,000
  • Total: $43,900–$105,600

ADU rental income potential: In high-demand markets (California, Pacific NW, major metros), a garage ADU can rent for $1,000–$2,500/month. At $1,500/month, a $70,000 ADU conversion pays for itself in under 4 years — then generates pure income. This is why ADUs are the hottest trend in residential construction right now. Many cities have streamlined ADU permitting specifically to encourage this.

5. Home Gym Conversion

Home gyms have surged in popularity, and a garage is the natural location. The conversion is similar to a home office — no plumbing required, and the concrete slab is actually an advantage for heavy equipment.

Home Gym Conversion Cost Breakdown (400 Sq Ft)

  • Garage door (keep or replace with insulated): $0–$2,500
  • Insulation (walls + ceiling): $1,200–$3,000
  • Drywall or wall panels: $1,200–$3,000
  • Rubber gym flooring (3/4" interlocking): $1,200–$2,400
  • Electrical (dedicated circuits, fan): $800–$2,000
  • HVAC (mini-split + ceiling fan): $2,000–$5,000
  • Mirrors (one wall): $400–$1,200
  • TV mount + wiring: $200–$500
  • Lighting (bright LED overhead): $300–$800
  • Paint: $300–$600
  • Total: $7,600–$21,000

Many gym conversions keep the garage door for ventilation and equipment loading. If you go this route, upgrade to an insulated garage door ($1,500–$2,500) for temperature control. Rubber gym flooring laid directly over the concrete slab is standard — it protects the slab from dropped weights and provides cushioning. Don't skip the HVAC; a garage gym without climate control is unusable for 6 months of the year in most climates.

6. Studio / Workshop Conversion

Studio/Workshop Conversion Cost Breakdown (400 Sq Ft)

  • Garage door (keep, replace, or wall off): $0–$3,500
  • Insulation: $1,200–$3,000
  • Walls (drywall, pegboard, or panel mix): $1,200–$3,500
  • Flooring (epoxy, LVP, or rubber): $1,000–$3,000
  • Electrical (240V outlet, 20-amp circuits, task lighting): $1,500–$4,000
  • HVAC: $1,500–$4,000
  • Utility sink (optional): $500–$1,500
  • Sound insulation (music studio): $2,000–$6,000
  • Paint & finishing: $400–$800
  • Permits: $500–$1,200
  • Total: $9,800–$30,500

Studios and workshops vary more than any other conversion type. An art studio needs great lighting and a utility sink. A music studio needs serious sound insulation (double drywall with Green Glue, MLV, decoupled walls). A woodworking shop needs 240V power and dust collection. Define the specific use first, then spec the conversion around it.

7. Component Cost Breakdown

No matter what type of conversion you're doing, these are the individual components and what they cost.

Insulation

  • Fiberglass batts (R-15 walls): $0.80–$1.50/sq ft
  • Rockwool batts (R-15 walls, better sound): $1.20–$2.00/sq ft
  • Spray foam (closed cell, R-21 walls): $2.50–$5.00/sq ft
  • Ceiling insulation (R-30 to R-38): $1.50–$3.50/sq ft
  • Total insulation (walls + ceiling, 400 sq ft garage): $1,200–$4,000

Flooring

  • Epoxy coating (over concrete): $3–$7/sq ft installed
  • Luxury vinyl plank (LVP): $4–$9/sq ft installed
  • Carpet: $3–$7/sq ft installed
  • Tile: $8–$16/sq ft installed
  • Rubber gym flooring: $3–$6/sq ft (DIY install)
  • Subfloor system (if raising floor level): $3–$6/sq ft additional

Important note about garage floors: Garage slabs are typically 4 inches lower than the house floor level. You have two options: build a raised subfloor ($1,200–$2,400 for 400 sq ft) to match the house level, or install a ramp/transition. For ADUs with a separate entrance, the step down isn't usually an issue. For conversions that connect to the house interior, matching floor heights creates a much better result.

HVAC Options

  • Extend existing ductwork: $1,000–$3,000 (if HVAC system has capacity)
  • Ductless mini-split (single zone): $2,500–$5,000 installed
  • Baseboard electric heat + window AC: $800–$2,000 (budget option)
  • Through-wall heat pump: $1,500–$3,500

Mini-split recommendation: For most garage conversions, a ductless mini-split is the best HVAC solution. It provides heating and cooling in one unit, doesn't require ductwork, and doesn't overload your existing HVAC system. A single-zone 12,000 BTU mini-split handles 400–500 sq ft and costs $2,500–$4,500 installed. It's worth the premium over space heaters and window units — especially for ADUs where tenant comfort matters.

8. Permits & Code Requirements

Every garage conversion requires permits. Period. Converting a garage from "storage" to "habitable space" triggers a long list of building code requirements.

Code Requirements for Habitable Garage Conversions

  • Ceiling height: Minimum 7'0" (some jurisdictions require 7'6")
  • Insulation: Must meet local energy code (R-15 walls, R-30+ ceiling typical)
  • Egress: Bedrooms require egress window (5.7 sq ft opening, sill ≤ 44")
  • Electrical: Must meet current residential code for habitable rooms
  • HVAC: Habitable rooms must have heating (and cooling in many states)
  • Fire separation: 5/8" Type X drywall on wall/ceiling between garage and house
  • Ventilation: Natural or mechanical ventilation per code
  • Smoke/CO detectors: Hardwired with battery backup
  • Floor: Must be finished (bare concrete may not qualify)
  • Parking replacement: Some cities require replacing lost parking spaces

Permit Costs

  • Basic conversion permit: $500–$1,500
  • ADU permit: $1,500–$5,000
  • Architectural plans (if required): $1,000–$5,000
  • Plan review fee: $200–$800
  • Impact fees (ADU, some jurisdictions): $0–$10,000

Don't skip the permit. Unpermitted garage conversions are the most commonly flagged issue in home inspections. When you sell, the buyer's inspector will catch it, and you'll be forced to either permit it retroactively (more expensive), restore the garage, or reduce the sale price. In some jurisdictions, unpermitted conversions trigger fines of $500–$5,000+ per day. Always pull permits.

9. What to Do With the Garage Door

The garage door opening is the defining decision of any conversion. You have three options:

Option 1: Frame a New Wall (Most Common)

  • Cost: $1,500–$3,500
  • Remove the garage door, frame a standard 2×6 wall with appropriate insulation
  • Add a window and/or entry door in the new wall
  • Match exterior finish (siding, stucco, brick) to the rest of the house
  • Exterior matching cost: $500–$2,000 additional
  • Best for: bedrooms, offices, ADUs — any conversion where you want the space to look like part of the house

Option 2: Keep the Garage Door

  • Cost: $0–$2,500 (for insulated upgrade)
  • Works for gyms, workshops, and studios where you want the option to open up
  • Upgrade to an insulated door for better climate control
  • Not ideal for bedrooms or living spaces (poor insulation, security, and sound)

Option 3: Replace With French Doors or Bifold Glass

  • Cost: $3,000–$8,000
  • Dramatic visual impact — floods the space with natural light
  • Great for studios, in-law suites, and ADUs
  • Requires structural header (most garage door openings already have one)

10. ROI & Property Value Impact

Does a garage conversion add or subtract value? It depends entirely on your market.

ROI by Conversion Type

  • ADU (rental unit) in high-demand market: 80–130% ROI
  • Additional bedroom + bath: 60–80% ROI
  • Home office: 50–70% ROI
  • Home gym: 40–60% ROI
  • Studio/workshop: 40–60% ROI

When garage conversion hurts value: In suburban and rural areas where 2-car garages are expected, removing garage parking can reduce property value by $5,000–$20,000. Buyers in these markets see a converted garage as a problem to fix, not an amenity. Before converting, check what comparable homes in your area sell for with and without garages.

When garage conversion helps value: In urban markets with housing shortages, ADUs are gold. California, Oregon, Washington, and many major cities have passed laws making ADU permitting easier. A $60,000 garage ADU that rents for $1,500/month provides both immediate income and long-term property value increase.

11. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Skipping the moisture barrier. Garage slabs often lack a vapor barrier underneath. Moisture wicking up through the concrete will destroy flooring and create mold. Test for moisture (tape plastic sheeting to the slab for 48 hours) and install a vapor barrier or moisture-resistant subfloor system before any finish flooring.
  2. Ignoring the floor height difference. The 3–4 inch step down from house to garage is a trip hazard and ADA concern. For connected conversions, build a raised subfloor. For separate-entrance ADUs, add a proper threshold and step.
  3. Forgetting exterior appearance. A walled-up garage door opening that doesn't match the house's siding, color, and trim screams "conversion" and hurts curb appeal. Spend the extra $500–$2,000 to match the exterior seamlessly.
  4. Inadequate HVAC. Garages have minimal insulation and lots of concrete thermal mass. A space heater won't cut it. Budget for a proper mini-split or duct extension — this is what makes the space livable year-round.
  5. No parking replacement plan. Some jurisdictions require you to replace the lost parking spaces (e.g., expand the driveway). Check before you permit. Even where not required, losing covered parking affects your home's appeal.
  6. Underestimating ADU complexity. A full ADU is not a weekend project. Kitchen and bathroom additions involve plumbing, electrical, HVAC, and fire separation — it's essentially building a small apartment. Budget and plan accordingly.

12. Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a garage conversion cost per square foot?

Basic conversions (bedroom, office) run $25–$55/sq ft. Mid-range with bathroom: $50–$100/sq ft. Full ADU: $100–$250/sq ft. The per-square-foot cost is high relative to the small space because fixed costs (permits, HVAC, garage door replacement) are spread across only 200–400 sq ft.

Can I convert just half of my garage?

Yes. Many homeowners convert one bay of a 2-car garage and keep the other for parking or storage. The partition wall adds $1,000–$2,500, and the remaining garage space keeps its utility. This is a smart compromise in markets where garage parking matters.

How long does a garage conversion take?

Simple conversion (bedroom/office): 2–4 weeks of construction. Conversion with bathroom: 4–6 weeks. Full ADU: 6–12 weeks. Add 2–8 weeks of permitting time before construction starts.

Do I need an architect for a garage conversion?

For simple conversions, a contractor can typically handle design and permitting without an architect. For ADUs, most jurisdictions require stamped architectural plans. Budget $1,000–$3,000 for a basic bedroom/office conversion plan or $2,000–$6,000 for ADU plans.

The Bottom Line

A garage conversion is one of the smartest ways to add living space without the cost and disruption of a full addition. You're starting with an existing structure — foundation, walls, roof — and upgrading it to habitable standards. That head start saves $30,000–$60,000 compared to building new square footage from scratch.

The key is matching the conversion type to your needs and budget. A bedroom conversion at $15,000 is a straightforward project that most competent contractors handle in 3 weeks. A full ADU at $70,000 is a major construction project that requires architect plans, multiple permits, and 3 months of work. Know which one you're signing up for before you start getting bids.

And always — always — pull permits. The money you save by skipping permits today becomes triple the money you spend when you try to sell.
