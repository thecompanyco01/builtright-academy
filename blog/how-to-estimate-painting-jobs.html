How to Estimate Painting Jobs: Pricing Formula + Examples

Most painting contractors price by gut feel and leave 20–40% of their profit on the table. Here's the exact formula professional painters use to estimate jobs — with real-world examples for interior and exterior work.

Painting is one of the most competitive trades. The barrier to entry is low, and homeowners are constantly comparing 3–5 bids. That means your estimating has to be tight — high enough to make real money, detailed enough to justify your price, and accurate enough that you don't eat costs on the job.

The painters making $80,000–$150,000+ per year aren't better with a brush than you. They're better at estimating. Let's fix that.

1. The Painting Estimate Formula

The Core Formula

Bid Price = (Materials + Labor) × Overhead Multiplier × Profit Multiplier

Or simplified:

  • Materials: Paint + primer + supplies (tape, caulk, drop cloths, sandpaper)
  • Labor: Hours × your loaded labor rate
  • Overhead multiplier: Typically 1.25–1.50 (covers vehicle, insurance, admin)
  • Profit multiplier: Typically 1.10–1.20 (your actual profit)

Combined, most professional painters use a total multiplier of 1.50–1.75 on their direct costs. If your direct costs (materials + labor) are $2,000, your bid should be $3,000–$3,500.

Quick sanity check: For residential repaints, your labor cost should be roughly 75–85% of the job and materials 15–25%. If your material costs are over 30% of the bid, you're probably undercharging for labor.

2. How to Measure for a Painting Estimate

Interior Walls

Measure the perimeter of each room and multiply by the ceiling height. This gives you gross wall square footage. Then subtract for windows and doors:

  • Standard door: Subtract 21 sq ft each
  • Standard window: Subtract 15 sq ft each
  • Sliding glass door: Subtract 40 sq ft each

Example: A 12' × 14' room with 9' ceilings, 1 door, 2 windows:

  • Perimeter: (12 + 14) × 2 = 52 linear feet
  • Gross wall area: 52 × 9 = 468 sq ft
  • Subtract openings: 468 - 21 - (15 × 2) = 417 sq ft of paintable wall

Ceilings

Length × width. Simple. A 12' × 14' room = 168 sq ft of ceiling.

Trim and Doors

Measure trim in linear feet. Baseboards, crown molding, chair rails, window casings, door casings. For pricing purposes, most painters estimate trim at 2–3× the time per linear foot compared to flat walls because of the detail work.

  • Baseboards: Measure linear feet around each room
  • Crown molding: Same as baseboards (same perimeter)
  • Door (both sides + jamb): Count as 45–60 sq ft equivalent each
  • Window trim: Count as 8–12 linear feet per window

Exterior

For exterior estimates, calculate the total siding area:

  • Measure each wall: width × height
  • For gable ends, calculate the triangle area separately
  • Subtract windows and doors
  • Measure fascia, soffit, and trim in linear feet
  • Note the siding type — lap siding, brick, stucco, wood shingle all paint at different rates

3. Calculating Material Costs

Paint Coverage

One gallon of quality paint covers approximately 350–400 sq ft on smooth surfaces with one coat. Adjust for:

  • Textured surfaces: Reduce coverage to 250–300 sq ft/gallon
  • Porous surfaces (new drywall, bare wood): First coat absorbs more — figure 250–300 sq ft/gallon
  • Dark-to-light color changes: Plan for 2–3 coats minimum
  • Standard repaint (similar color): 2 coats

Material Price List (2026 Averages)

Interior Materials

  • Premium interior paint (Sherwin-Williams, Benjamin Moore): $45–$75/gallon
  • Primer: $25–$45/gallon
  • Caulk: $4–$8/tube (budget 1 tube per 2 rooms)
  • Painter's tape: $6–$10/roll
  • Drop cloths, sandpaper, patching compound: $30–$60 per job
  • Roller covers, brushes: $15–$30 per job

Exterior Materials

  • Premium exterior paint: $50–$80/gallon
  • Exterior primer: $30–$50/gallon
  • Caulk (exterior grade): $6–$12/tube
  • Pressure washer rental (if needed): $75–$150/day
  • Scraper blades, wire brushes: $20–$40

Pro tip: Always use premium paint on your estimates. Cheap paint ($25/gallon) requires more coats, covers worse, and looks bad after 2 years. Your reputation rides on the finished product. Premium paint actually saves you labor time and generates better reviews.

4. Estimating Labor Hours (Production Rates)

This is where most estimating mistakes happen. Here are realistic production rates for a skilled painter working at a sustainable pace:

Interior Production Rates

Walls (Per Coat)

  • Rolling walls (open rooms): 150–200 sq ft/hour
  • Cutting in (edges, corners): 60–80 linear ft/hour
  • Walls with lots of trim/obstacles: 100–130 sq ft/hour

Trim and Detail Work

  • Baseboards: 40–60 linear ft/hour
  • Crown molding: 30–50 linear ft/hour
  • Doors (both sides + jamb): 1–1.5 hours each
  • Window trim: 30–45 minutes each
  • Cabinets: 3–6 hours per average kitchen (brush/roll method)

Prep Work (Don't Forget This!)

  • Light prep (clean walls, fill small holes): Add 15–20% to paint time
  • Medium prep (patch larger holes, sand, caulk): Add 25–35% to paint time
  • Heavy prep (wallpaper removal, extensive patching, priming): Add 50–100% to paint time

Exterior Production Rates

  • Smooth siding (spray): 400–600 sq ft/hour
  • Lap siding (brush/roll): 80–120 sq ft/hour
  • Trim work: 30–50 linear ft/hour
  • Pressure washing: 500–1,000 sq ft/hour
  • Scraping and sanding: 30–80 sq ft/hour depending on condition

Your Loaded Labor Rate

Your labor rate isn't just what you pay per hour. It's the fully loaded cost:

  • Solo painter (owner): $35–$55/hour loaded cost (before profit)
  • Employee painter: Wage × 1.25–1.35 (payroll taxes, workers comp, benefits)
  • Subcontract painter: Whatever they charge you

5. Adding Overhead and Profit

After calculating direct costs (materials + labor), apply your multipliers:

Typical Overhead Items for Painters

  • Vehicle costs: $600–$1,200/month
  • Insurance (general liability + workers comp): $200–$600/month
  • Marketing and website: $200–$500/month
  • Equipment (sprayers, ladders, scaffolding amortized): $100–$300/month
  • Phone, software, admin time: $100–$200/month

Add up your monthly overhead, divide by your monthly revenue, and that's your overhead percentage. Most painting contractors run 25–40% overhead.

Then add profit. Target 10–20% net profit. Don't skip this — overhead covers costs, profit is what you actually keep.

6. Full Example: Interior Repaint (3-Bedroom Home)

Let's walk through a complete estimate for a common job: repainting the interior of a 1,500 sq ft 3-bedroom, 2-bath home. Walls and ceilings, all rooms. Standard repaint — similar colors, walls in decent condition.

Measurements

  • Total wall area: ~2,800 sq ft
  • Total ceiling area: ~1,500 sq ft
  • Doors to paint: 10 (both sides)
  • Baseboards: 380 linear ft
  • Window trim: 12 windows

Materials

  • Wall paint (2 coats): 2,800 sq ft ÷ 375 sq ft/gal × 2 coats = 15 gallons × $55 = $825
  • Ceiling paint (1 coat): 1,500 ÷ 400 = 4 gallons × $45 = $180
  • Trim paint (2 coats): 3 gallons × $60 = $180
  • Primer (spot): 1 gallon = $35
  • Supplies (caulk, tape, patching, rollers): $85
  • Total materials: $1,305

Labor

  • Prep work (light-medium): 8 hours
  • Ceilings: 8 hours
  • Walls (cut in + roll, 2 coats): 22 hours
  • Trim/baseboards: 12 hours
  • Doors (10 × 1.25 hrs): 12.5 hours
  • Windows: 6 hours
  • Setup/cleanup/movement: 6 hours
  • Total labor: 74.5 hours × $45/hr = $3,352

Final Bid Calculation

  • Direct costs: $1,305 + $3,352 = $4,657
  • Overhead (30%): $1,397
  • Subtotal: $6,054
  • Profit (15%): $908
  • Bid price: $6,962 → Quote at $7,000

A solo painter could complete this job in about 9–10 working days. A crew of two could do it in 5 days. That $7,000 bid yields roughly $900 in profit — which is about $100/day net. If you're faster than average, your profit goes up.

7. Full Example: Exterior Repaint (2-Story Colonial)

Measurements

  • Siding area: ~2,200 sq ft (lap siding, fair condition)
  • Fascia/soffit: 180 linear ft
  • Window and door trim: 16 windows, 2 doors
  • Shutters: 12
  • Front door: 1 (special finish)

Materials

  • Exterior paint (2 coats body): 2,200 ÷ 350 × 2 = 13 gallons × $65 = $845
  • Trim paint: 3 gallons × $65 = $195
  • Primer (bare/peeling areas): 2 gallons × $40 = $80
  • Caulk (exterior): 8 tubes × $8 = $64
  • Supplies (scrapers, sandpaper, drop cloths): $75
  • Total materials: $1,259

Labor

  • Pressure washing: 4 hours
  • Scraping and sanding: 12 hours
  • Caulking: 6 hours
  • Priming bare spots: 4 hours
  • Body paint (2 coats, brush/roll): 28 hours
  • Trim: 14 hours
  • Shutters: 8 hours
  • Setup/cleanup/ladder movement: 8 hours
  • Total labor: 84 hours × $45/hr = $3,780

Final Bid

  • Direct costs: $1,259 + $3,780 = $5,039
  • Overhead (30%): $1,512
  • Subtotal: $6,551
  • Profit (15%): $983
  • Bid price: $7,534 → Quote at $7,500

Exterior pricing note: Exterior work is higher-risk (weather delays, ladder work, more prep) and should generally carry a higher profit margin. Many painters use 18–20% profit on exterior jobs vs. 12–15% on interior.

8. Price Adjustments for Common Scenarios

Add More When:

  • Color change (dark to light): Add 20–30% to labor for extra coats
  • High ceilings (over 10'): Add 15–25% for scaffold setup and slower pace
  • Heavy prep (wallpaper removal, extensive drywall repair): Estimate prep separately — it can double the job
  • Older homes: Add 15–20% — more patching, more caulking, more surprises
  • Occupied homes: Add 10–15% — moving furniture, plastic coverage, working around people
  • Lead paint (pre-1978 homes): Add 30–50% for proper containment, EPA RRP compliance
  • Multi-story exterior: Add 15–25% per story above the first for ladder/scaffold time

Volume Discounts

For whole-house repaints or multi-unit jobs, you can reduce your price per square foot because:

  • One setup/cleanup instead of multiple
  • Better material pricing on bulk paint orders
  • More efficient workflow

A reasonable volume discount is 5–10%. Don't go deeper — you still need to be profitable.

9. Estimating Mistakes That Kill Painting Profits

  1. Underestimating prep time. This is the #1 profit killer for painters. Prep is 30–50% of most paint jobs. If you're only budgeting 10% for prep, you're losing money on every project.
  2. Not doing a site visit. Photos from the homeowner don't show peeling paint, patching needs, or access issues. Always visit. Always measure yourself.
  3. Pricing per square foot without adjusting. "$2/sq ft for interior painting" sounds simple, but a room with 12' ceilings, crown molding, and dark walls is 3x the work of a standard room. Flat per-sq-ft pricing kills your margins on complex jobs.
  4. Forgetting to account for coats. Two coats takes roughly 80% more time than one coat (second coat goes faster). Three coats takes about 150% more. Factor this in.
  5. Quoting over the phone. "How much to paint my living room?" is not a question you can answer without seeing it. Train yourself to say: "I'd love to give you an accurate number. When can I come take a look?"
  6. Matching the cheapest bid. If someone got a quote for $3,000 and you're at $5,000, explain the difference — don't drop your price. The cheap painter is either cutting corners, underestimating, or going out of business.
  7. Not charging for extras. Homeowner wants you to patch 15 drywall holes and remove wallpaper in the bathroom "while you're here"? That's a separate line item, not a freebie.

The Bottom Line

Estimating painting jobs isn't about pulling a number out of the air or matching what the other guy charges. It's about knowing your numbers: square footage, production rates, material costs, overhead, and profit.

The formula works: Materials + Labor + Overhead + Profit = Your Price. Every time.

The painters who use a system like this consistently earn 2–3x more than those who wing it. Not because they charge outrageously — because they stop giving money away on every job.

Measure twice. Estimate once. Profit every time.
