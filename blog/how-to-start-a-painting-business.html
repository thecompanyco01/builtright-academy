How to Start a Painting Business — Complete Guide (2026)

Painting is one of the easiest, most profitable trade businesses to start. Low startup costs, massive demand, and margins that make other trades jealous. Whether you want to paint houses or commercial buildings, this guide covers everything from your first brush stroke to your first crew.

1. Why a Painting Business Works

Painting might seem simple, but the business fundamentals are outstanding:

  • Extremely low startup costs: You can launch a painting business for under $3,000 — less than almost any other trade. No expensive specialized equipment, no mandatory certifications (in most states).
  • High profit margins: Paint and supplies cost 15–25% of the job price. Labor is your biggest cost, and when you're the labor, most of the revenue is profit. Even with employees, 25–40% net margins are normal.
  • Massive, recurring market: Interior paint lasts 5–10 years. Exterior paint lasts 5–7 years. Every painted surface eventually needs repainting. That's 140+ million housing units in the US cycling through repaint schedules.
  • Year-round work: Interior painting is weather-independent. Even in cold climates, you can stay busy year-round by focusing on interiors in winter and exteriors in summer.
  • Easy to learn, hard to master: The basics of painting are straightforward. But quality prep work, clean lines, speed, and customer service separate professionals from amateurs. This skill gap is your moat.
  • Quick cash flow: Most residential painting jobs are 1–5 days. You invoice and collect quickly. No waiting months for payment like some construction trades.

The real money in painting: It's not in the paint — it's in the prep. Homeowners pay premium prices for meticulous preparation: washing, scraping, sanding, caulking, priming, patching. A $3,000 exterior paint job might include $500 in paint and $2,500 in labor, with 60% of that labor being prep. If your prep is excellent, your paint job will look incredible and customers will refer you endlessly.

2. Residential vs. Commercial Painting

Residential Painting

  • Job sizes: $1,500–$8,000 for interiors, $3,000–$15,000 for exteriors
  • Sales cycle: Short — homeowner decides in 1–2 weeks typically
  • Marketing: Local SEO, referrals, Nextdoor, yard signs
  • Pros: Lower insurance requirements, smaller crew needed, faster payment, higher margins
  • Cons: Smaller job sizes, more customer hand-holding, scheduling around homeowner availability

Commercial Painting

  • Job sizes: $5,000–$100,000+ (office buildings, apartment complexes, retail spaces)
  • Sales cycle: Longer — competitive bidding, multiple decision-makers
  • Marketing: Relationships with property managers, GCs, and facility managers
  • Pros: Larger jobs, more predictable scheduling, potential for recurring contracts
  • Cons: Lower margins (competitive bidding), higher insurance requirements, need for larger crews

Recommendation: Start with residential. Lower risk, easier to win jobs, faster learning cycle. Move into commercial once you have a crew, systems, and insurance to support it.

3. Licensing & Legal Setup

Painting has some of the lightest licensing requirements of any trade, but you still need to handle the basics:

State Licensing Requirements

State License Required? Notes

Texas No state license Local business license only
Florida No specific painting license General contractor license needed for larger projects
New York Varies by locality NYC requires Home Improvement Contractor license
Arizona Yes — C-33 Painting & Decorating Required for jobs over $1,000
Georgia No state license Local business license required

Check our state-by-state licensing guide for your specific requirements.

Critical: EPA Lead Paint Certification (RRP). If you work on ANY home built before 1978, you MUST be EPA Lead-Safe Certified (RRP — Renovation, Repair, and Painting rule). This requires an 8-hour training course ($200–$300) and specific work practices. Violations carry fines up to $37,500 per day. This is federal law — it applies in every state. Don't skip this.

Business Formation Checklist

  • LLC: File in your state ($50–$500). Protects your personal assets.
  • EIN: Free from IRS.gov. Takes 5 minutes.
  • Business license: From your city/county ($50–$200).
  • Business bank account: Keep personal and business money separate.
  • Accounting software: Wave (free) or QuickBooks Self-Employed ($15/month).
  • EPA RRP certification: If working on pre-1978 homes ($200–$300 one-time).

5. Equipment & Supplies

Essential Startup Kit

  • Quality brush set (2", 2.5", 3" angled sash brushes) — $40–$80. Purdy or Wooster. Don't cheap out on brushes.
  • Roller frames (9" and 18") — $20–$50
  • Roller covers (3/8" for smooth walls, 1/2" for textured, 3/4" for rough surfaces) — $50–$100 (buy in bulk)
  • Extension poles (2–4 foot and 4–8 foot) — $30–$60
  • Drop cloths (canvas, not plastic) — $50–$100
  • Painter's tape (FrogTape or 3M blue) — $30–$60
  • 5-in-1 tool — $10–$15. The most useful painting tool ever invented.
  • Putty knives (1.5", 3", 6") — $15–$30
  • Caulk gun + paintable caulk — $20–$40
  • Sanding supplies (pole sander, sanding blocks, sandpaper) — $30–$60
  • Paint can opener, stir sticks, strainers — $10–$20
  • 6-foot step ladder — $80–$150 (fiberglass)
  • Extension ladder (24' or 28') — $150–$350
  • Bucket, grid, and liner — $15–$25

Level Up (Add After First 10 Jobs)

  • Airless paint sprayer (Graco Magnum or similar) — $300–$800. A game-changer for exterior work and large interiors. One sprayer can 3x your production speed.
  • Pressure washer — $200–$400. Essential for exterior prep work.
  • Paint edger tool — $10–$20 (for cutting in faster)
  • Scaffolding — $200–$600 (for tall exterior walls and stairwells)
  • Heat gun/paint scraper set — $50–$100 (for lead paint or heavy paint buildup)

6. How to Estimate Painting Jobs

Accurate estimating separates profitable painters from struggling ones. Here's a systematic approach. Also see our detailed painting estimating guide.

Step 1: Measure Everything

Measure the square footage of every surface to be painted:

  • Walls: Length × height for each wall. Subtract windows and doors.
  • Ceilings: Length × width of each room.
  • Trim: Measure linear feet of baseboards, crown molding, door/window casings.
  • Doors: Count each door (interior doors average 20 sq ft per side).

Step 2: Assess Prep Work

Prep is where most estimating errors happen. Check for:

  • Holes and dents that need patching
  • Peeling or flaking paint that needs scraping
  • Caulking gaps around trim, windows, doors
  • Surfaces that need sanding or priming
  • Wallpaper removal (add 2–4 hours per room)
  • Lead paint (pre-1978 homes — requires RRP procedures)

Step 3: Calculate Costs

Service Price Range (Per Sq Ft) Notes
Interior walls (brush/roller) $1.50–$3.50 Includes 2 coats, basic prep
Interior walls (spray) $1.00–$2.50 Faster but more masking required
Ceilings $1.00–$2.50 Add 20% for textured ceilings
Trim/baseboards (per linear foot) $1.50–$3.00 Detailed work, slower production
Exterior siding $1.50–$4.00 Includes power wash, scrape, prime, 2 coats
Exterior trim $2.00–$4.00/LF Detailed work, often requires ladder
Cabinets (per linear foot) $30–$60 High-margin specialty work
Doors (each) $75–$200 Per side, depends on detail level

Step 4: Build the Estimate

  1. Materials (paint + supplies): typically 15–25% of total
  2. Labor: your time or your crew's time at your hourly cost
  3. Overhead: 10–15% (insurance, vehicle, admin)
  4. Profit margin: 25–40%

Pro tip: always quote by the project, not by the hour. Hourly billing punishes you for being fast and efficient. If you can paint a room in 4 hours that takes your competitor 8 hours, you should earn MORE per hour, not the same. Quote a project price based on the value to the customer, then work efficiently to maximize your effective hourly rate.

7. Hiring Painters

When you're ready to grow beyond solo work, hiring painters is your next step. Here's how to do it right:

What to Look For

  • Prep work quality: The most important skill. Anyone can roll paint. Not everyone can patch, sand, caulk, and mask properly.
  • Cutting in ability: Can they cut a clean line without tape? This separates professionals from amateurs.
  • Speed: A fast, clean painter is worth 2 slow painters. Production rate matters enormously.
  • Reliability: Shows up on time, every day. In the painting trade, reliability is rarer than skill.
  • Cleanliness: Keeps the job site clean, doesn't drip paint everywhere, protects floors and furniture.

Pay Structure

  • Hourly: $15–$25/hour for helpers, $20–$35/hour for experienced painters
  • Per job/piece rate: Many painting companies pay by the job or by the room. This incentivizes speed and lets experienced painters earn more.
  • Subcontractors: Some painters prefer 1099 arrangements. Be careful — the IRS has strict rules about who qualifies as a contractor vs. employee. If you control when, where, and how they work, they're likely an employee.

8. Marketing & Getting Customers

1. Google Business Profile (Most Important)

Set up your GBP with high-quality photos of completed work (before/after shots are gold). Post weekly updates showing recent projects. Respond to every review. "Painters near me" is one of the highest-volume local searches in the trades — you want to rank for it.

2. Before/After Photos on Social Media

Painting is one of the most visual trades — the transformation is dramatic and shareable. Post before/after photos on Instagram, Facebook, and Nextdoor. Use location hashtags (#[YourCity]Painter). These posts generate organic engagement and leads. Before/after content performs 3–5x better than regular posts.

3. Referral Program

Painting has one of the highest referral rates of any trade because the results are immediately visible to visitors. Offer $50–$100 per referral that becomes a paid job. Leave extra business cards at every completed job. Happy customers sell your services for you.

4. Nextdoor & Local Groups

Nextdoor is huge for painters. Create a business page, encourage customer recommendations, and engage with neighborhood posts. When someone asks "who knows a good painter?" — that's your cue. Facebook community groups work similarly.

More Marketing Ideas

  • Door hangers: Target neighborhoods with homes that clearly need exterior repainting. Include a before/after photo and a specific offer.
  • Partner with realtors: Offer pre-sale painting packages for homes going on the market. A fresh paint job increases sale price by $2,000–$5,000 — easy sell for agents.
  • Property management companies: Apartments and commercial properties need regular repainting. Land one property management contract and you could have work for months. Learn more in our contractor marketing guide.
  • Color consultation: Offer free color consultations to differentiate from competitors. Help homeowners choose colors using fan decks and large samples. This builds trust and closes deals.

9. Scaling From Solo to Crew

The Growth Path

  1. Solo (Month 1–6): Do everything yourself. Learn every aspect of the business. Build your reputation and system.
  2. Solo + helper (Month 6–12): Hire one helper for prep work and rolling. You focus on cutting, detail work, and estimates.
  3. One crew (Year 1–2): 2–3 painters working together. You split time between working and running the business (estimating, scheduling, customer communication).
  4. Multiple crews (Year 2+): You step off the ladder entirely and focus on sales, estimating, and business management. Each crew has a lead painter who handles quality on site.

When to Stop Painting and Start Managing

The hardest transition in any painting business is when the owner stops painting and starts managing. Here's the rule of thumb: when you have more estimates to do than hours in the day, and you're turning away work because you can't get to it, it's time to hire and manage.

Many painters resist this because they enjoy the work. But your highest-value activity is selling and managing, not rolling walls. A $5,000 job you sell and manage in 2 hours of your time is more profitable than a $3,000 job you spend 30 hours painting yourself.

10. Common Mistakes

  1. Skimping on prep work.

    Every callback, every complaint, every negative review traces back to inadequate prep. Rushing through prep to save time always costs more in the end. Allocate 30–50% of job time to preparation — washing, scraping, sanding, caulking, priming, patching. This is what separates $50/hour painters from $25/hour painters.

  2. Underestimating jobs.

    The #1 profit killer. Walk every job carefully. Check every wall, every window, every piece of trim. Look at the condition of existing paint. A room that "just needs two coats" might actually need scraping, patching, priming, AND two coats — doubling your time. Always estimate on the conservative (higher) side.

  3. Using cheap paint.

    Budget paint costs $20–$30/gallon. Premium paint costs $40–$60/gallon. The price difference on a $4,000 job is $50–$100. But premium paint covers better (fewer coats), goes on smoother, lasts longer, and looks better. Your customers will see the difference, and you'll do less rework. Use Sherwin-Williams, Benjamin Moore, or equivalent. Never use contractor-grade paint.

  4. Not having a written estimate.

    Always provide a detailed written estimate that specifies: number of coats, paint brand and sheen, what's included (and NOT included), prep work scope, and price. "I thought you were painting the trim too" disputes are 100% preventable with a clear scope of work.

  5. Ignoring the EPA Lead Paint Rule.

    Working on pre-1978 homes without RRP certification is a federal violation with fines up to $37,500 per day. Beyond the legal risk, lead dust exposure is a real health hazard for you, your crew, and the homeowner's family. Get certified. Follow the rules.

  6. No photos of completed work.

    Every job is a marketing opportunity. Take professional-quality before/after photos of EVERY project. These photos become your portfolio, your social media content, your Google Business Profile photos, and your most powerful sales tool. A picture of a beautifully painted room sells better than any brochure.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to start a painting business?

A painting business can be started for $2,000–$6,500, making it one of the cheapest trade businesses to launch. Core costs: equipment ($650–$2,000), insurance ($500–$1,500/year), business registration ($100–$500), and marketing ($200–$1,000). If you own a vehicle and basic ladders, you can start for under $1,500.

Do I need a license?

Most states don't require a specific painting license for residential work below a dollar threshold. However, you always need a general business license. States like California and Arizona require painting contractor licenses for jobs above $500–$1,000. The EPA requires RRP certification for work on pre-1978 homes in every state.

How much do painters charge per square foot?

Interior painting typically runs $1.50–$3.50 per square foot of wall surface. Exterior painting runs $1.50–$4.00/sq ft. These rates include labor, paint, and supplies. Specialty work like cabinets or detailed trim commands higher rates. Prices vary significantly by region — urban areas run 30–50% higher than rural markets.

How much can a painting business make?

A solo painter typically earns $40,000–$80,000/year. A business with 2–4 employees can generate $200,000–$500,000 in annual revenue with 25–40% net margins. The key to higher income: efficient estimating, consistent marketing, quality work that generates referrals, and eventually transitioning from painter to business owner/manager.

Residential or commercial — which is better?

Start residential. Lower barriers, smaller jobs (less financial risk), easier marketing, and higher margins. Move to commercial once you have a reliable crew, proven systems, and higher insurance limits. Many successful painting companies do both — residential for higher margins and commercial for volume and predictability.

How do I estimate painting jobs?

Measure all surfaces, assess prep work needed, calculate paint quantities, estimate labor hours based on production rates (200–400 sq ft/hour for walls), add materials + labor + overhead (10–15%) + profit (25–40%). Always quote by the project, not by the hour — it rewards your efficiency. Use our paint calculator for quick material estimates.

What equipment do I need to start?

Essential kit: quality brushes (Purdy/Wooster), roller frames and covers, extension poles, drop cloths, painter's tape, putty knives, caulk gun, sanding supplies, and ladders (step + extension). Total: $650–$1,600. Add a paint sprayer ($300–$800) after your first 10 jobs — it dramatically increases production speed on larger projects.

Should I use a paint sprayer or brush/roller?

Both. Use brush and roller for occupied interiors (less masking, less overspray, homeowners prefer it). Use a sprayer for exteriors, new construction, and large empty interiors. A sprayer can cover 3–5x more area per hour but requires more setup/masking time. Most professional painters use both techniques depending on the job.

The Bottom Line

A painting business is one of the best first trade businesses you can start. The startup costs are minimal, the learning curve is manageable, and the profit margins are outstanding. You can be painting your first paid job within a week of deciding to start.

The painters who build real businesses — $200K, $500K, $1M+ in revenue — are the ones who obsess over prep work, price for profit (not just to win bids), market consistently, and eventually transition from doing the work to running the business.

You don't need perfection. You need a brush, a ladder, insurance, and the willingness to knock on some doors. Start painting. Start getting paid. Start building your reputation one wall at a time.
