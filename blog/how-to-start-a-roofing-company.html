How to Start a Roofing Company — Complete Guide (2026)

Roofing is one of the most profitable trades you can get into. Average job tickets run $8,000–$15,000 for a residential re-roof, margins are healthy, and demand never stops — every roof eventually needs replacing. Here's how to start a roofing company the right way.

1. Why Roofing Is a Great Business in 2026

The US roofing market is worth over $56 billion annually and growing. Here's why it's one of the best trade businesses to start:

  • High ticket prices: A single residential re-roof generates $8,000–$15,000+ in revenue. You don't need hundreds of customers to build a $500K business — you need 40–60 jobs per year.
  • Recurring demand: Asphalt shingle roofs last 20–30 years. With over 80 million single-family homes in the US, millions of roofs need replacement every year — and storms create additional emergency demand.
  • Strong margins: Well-run roofing companies maintain 25–40% profit margins on residential work. Few trades offer margins this healthy on jobs this large.
  • Storm-driven demand spikes: Hail storms, hurricanes, and high winds create massive, sudden demand in specific markets. Roofers who respond quickly to storm events can do a full year's revenue in 2–3 months.
  • Scalable: Unlike many trades, roofing scales well. Add crews, add volume. The business model supports going from solo operator to multi-crew operation to multi-location company.
  • Insurance-paid work: A significant portion of residential roofing is paid by homeowners' insurance. This means customers are less price-sensitive — the insurance company is writing the check.

The real opportunity: The roofing industry has a massive reputation problem. Homeowners are terrified of getting scammed by fly-by-night roofers. If you build a legitimate, professional company — proper licensing, insurance, warranties, clean trucks, uniformed crews — you'll stand out from 80% of your competition just by being trustworthy.

2. Licensing & Legal Requirements

Roofing is one of the more heavily regulated trades. Most states require a contractor license to perform roofing work, and many have specific roofing contractor classifications.

Check our state-by-state contractor licensing guide for specific requirements in your state.

Business Formation Essentials

  • LLC or Corporation: Form an LLC at minimum. Roofing has significant liability exposure — you need the legal protection. File in your state ($50–$500).
  • EIN: Get your federal Employer Identification Number from IRS.gov (free, takes 5 minutes).
  • Contractor bond: Many states require a surety bond ($5,000–$25,000 face value). The bond costs 1–5% of the face value annually ($50–$1,250/year).
  • Business bank account: Absolutely required. Never mix personal and business funds — especially in roofing where job deposits can be $3,000–$5,000.
  • Building permits: Learn your local permit requirements. In most jurisdictions, roof replacements require a building permit ($100–$500 per job).

Roofing requires more startup capital than many trades because of insurance costs and equipment needs. Here's a realistic breakdown:

Expense Cost Range Notes

Equipment & tools $5,000–$15,000 See equipment list below
Truck & trailer $5,000–$25,000 Used 3/4-ton truck + flatbed trailer
Marketing (initial) $1,000–$5,000 Website, Google Ads, yard signs, vehicle wrap

Working capital $5,000–$15,000 Materials for first 2–3 jobs before payment comes in

The lean startup approach: If $25K+ is too steep, start as a roofing subcontractor. Work under an established company's license and insurance. You provide the labor crew, they provide the customer, materials, and permits. You'll earn less per job but learn the business with almost zero startup cost. After 1–2 years, you'll have the experience, savings, and contacts to launch your own company.

4. Insurance Requirements

Roofing is classified as high-risk by insurance companies because of the height, heavy materials, and potential for property damage. This means your premiums will be significantly higher than most other trades.

Insurance Type Annual Cost Why You Need It
General Liability ($1M/$2M) $3,000–$7,000 Covers property damage and bodily injury. Required by most states and all serious customers.
Workers' Compensation $5,000–$15,000 Required in nearly every state once you hire employees. Roofing has high workers' comp rates due to injury risk.
Commercial Auto $1,500–$3,000 Covers your trucks and trailers used for business.
Inland Marine (Tools/Equipment) $300–$800 Covers equipment theft and damage. Important when you have $15K+ in tools.
Umbrella Policy $500–$1,500 Extra liability coverage above your GL limits. Worth it for roofing.

Total insurance cost: $10,000–$27,000/year. This is your biggest fixed cost. Factor it into every bid. On a $10,000 re-roof, roughly $500–$1,000 goes to insurance costs.

6. Hiring Your First Crew

Roofing is a crew business. Unlike a handyman or painter who can work solo, most roofing jobs require 3–5 workers minimum to be efficient and safe.

Crew Structure

  • Crew lead/foreman: Your most experienced roofer. Manages the job site, ensures quality, handles unexpected issues. Pay: $25–$40/hour.
  • Experienced roofers (2–3): Can install shingles, flash chimneys, install drip edge, and work valleys. Pay: $18–$30/hour.
  • Laborers (1–2): Handle tear-off, cleanup, carrying materials, and ground support. Pay: $15–$22/hour.

Where to Find Workers

  • Word of mouth: The #1 source. Ask other contractors, go to supply houses, post on community boards.
  • Indeed/Craigslist: Post clear job listings with pay ranges and experience requirements.
  • Day labor connections: Many experienced roofers find work through community networks. Build relationships in your local labor community.
  • Trade schools: Contact local trade schools for graduates looking for work.

The labor reality: Finding reliable roofing crews is the #1 challenge in this business. The work is physically demanding, hot, and dangerous. Pay well, treat people right, and provide consistent work. A good crew is worth more than any piece of equipment you'll ever own. Roofers who job-hop are looking for better pay and better treatment — be the company they want to stay at.

7. How to Bid Roofing Jobs

Accurate estimating is the difference between a profitable roofing company and one that goes broke. Here's how to price roofing jobs correctly.

The Measurement

Roofing is measured in squares (1 square = 100 square feet). A typical residential roof is 20–35 squares. You can measure using:

  • Manual measurement: Ladder up, tape measure, calculate (old school but accurate)
  • Satellite measurement tools: EagleView, RoofSnap, or Google Earth. These cost $15–$50 per report but save hours and improve accuracy.
  • Drone measurement: Fly your own drone, generate 3D models. Most accurate method.

Architectural shingles (material) $100–$150 Industry standard, most common
Premium/designer shingles (material) $150–$350 Higher margin, fewer competitors install these
Underlayment & ice/water shield $15–$40 Required by code in most areas

Accessories (ridge, starter, vents) $20–$40 Ridge caps, starter strip, pipe boots, vents
Dump fees $15–$30 Dumpster rental split across the job

Total cost per square: $305–$520. Add your profit margin (25–40%) on top. A 25-square architectural shingle re-roof might cost you $8,000–$10,000 and sell for $11,000–$14,000.

8. Marketing & Getting Customers

The Fastest Customer Acquisition Channels

1. Storm Chasing (Fastest Revenue)

After significant hail or wind events, homeowners need roof repairs and replacements — fast. Monitor weather patterns, drive through affected neighborhoods, knock doors, and offer free inspections. This is how many roofing companies build their entire first year of revenue. Be professional, be honest about damage, and help homeowners navigate the insurance process.

2. Google Business Profile + SEO

Set up your Google Business Profile immediately. Get your first 10–15 reviews by offering a small discount to early customers who leave honest reviews. Homeowners searching "roofing company near me" are high-intent buyers — they need a roof NOW. Ranking in the local map pack for this search can generate 10–30 leads per month. Also check our guide on roofing marketing strategies.

3. Real Estate Agent Partnerships

Real estate agents need roof inspections for every home sale. Offer free pre-listing roof inspections for agents in your area. When the inspection reveals issues, you're already positioned as the trusted roofer to do the work. One productive real estate agent relationship can generate 5–15 jobs per year.

4. Door Knocking

Drive through neighborhoods with older roofs (20+ year old subdivisions). Knock doors and offer free inspections. This is direct, uncomfortable for most people, and incredibly effective. A good door knocker can set 3–5 inspection appointments per day. Even if only 30% convert, that's 1–2 new jobs per day of knocking.

Secondary Marketing Channels

  • Yard signs: Put a branded sign in every customer's yard during the project. Neighbors see the work happening and call. This costs almost nothing and generates steady leads.
  • Vehicle wraps: A professional truck wrap ($2,000–$4,000) turns your vehicle into a moving billboard. Worth every penny for a roofing company.
  • Home shows and events: Set up a booth at local home improvement shows. Great for building brand awareness and collecting leads.
  • Referral program: Offer $250–$500 per referral that becomes a signed contract. Happy customers are your best salespeople.

9. Working With Insurance Claims

A large percentage of residential roofing work — especially in storm-prone areas — is paid through homeowners' insurance claims. Understanding this process is critical to your success.

The Insurance Claim Process

  1. Inspect the roof and document damage (photos, measurements, notes).
  2. Help the homeowner file a claim with their insurance company.
  3. Meet the insurance adjuster on the roof. Walk the damage with them. Point out everything — hail hits, wind damage, missing shingles, damaged flashing.
  4. Review the adjuster's scope of work. If they missed items, file a supplement (additional documentation requesting more coverage).
  5. Get approval and schedule the work.
  6. Complete the work and collect payment (insurance check + homeowner's deductible).

Ethics matter enormously here. Never fabricate or exaggerate damage. Never offer to "waive" the homeowner's deductible (this is insurance fraud in most states). Never pressure homeowners into signing contracts before the adjuster visits. The roofing industry has a terrible reputation because of companies that do these things. Be the company that does it right — it's better for your customers, your reputation, and your long-term business.

10. Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Underbidding to win jobs.

    New roofing companies often price too low to win work, then lose money on every job. Know your actual costs (materials + labor + overhead + insurance) and add a real profit margin. Winning a $10,000 job that costs you $10,500 to complete is worse than losing the bid.

  2. Skipping workers' compensation insurance.

    Roofing is dangerous. If an uninsured worker falls and gets injured on your job site, you're personally liable for medical bills, lost wages, and lawsuits. This can — and does — bankrupt roofing companies. Workers' comp is expensive but non-negotiable.

  3. Neglecting safety and OSHA compliance.

    OSHA fines for fall protection violations on roofing sites run $15,000–$150,000+ per violation. Beyond the financial risk, people get seriously hurt and killed on roofs every year. Invest in fall protection equipment, train your crew, and enforce safety rules. No job is worth a life.

  4. No written contracts.

    Every roofing job needs a written contract specifying: scope of work, materials, price, payment terms, warranty, start/completion dates, and what happens if additional work is needed. Handshake deals lead to disputes, non-payment, and lawsuits.

  5. Growing too fast.

    Adding crews before you have the systems, cash flow, and management capacity to support them. One good crew doing 3–4 jobs per week is more profitable than three mediocre crews doing sloppy work. Scale deliberately.

  6. Ignoring manufacturer certification.

    Get certified by major shingle manufacturers (GAF, Owens Corning, CertainTeed). Certification lets you offer extended warranties that unlicensed competitors can't match. It's a major selling point and costs little beyond attending a training class.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does it cost to start a roofing company?

Starting a roofing company typically costs $15,000–$50,000 for a properly set-up operation with insurance, equipment, licensing, and working capital. You can start leaner ($10,000–$15,000) if you already own a truck and work as a sub-contractor initially. The biggest cost is insurance — expect $10,000–$25,000/year for GL + workers' comp.

Do I need a contractor license?

Most states require a contractor license to perform roofing work. Some have specific roofing classifications (California's C-39, Arizona's CR-42), while others require a general contractor license. A few states like Texas and Colorado have no state-level licensing but may have local requirements. Check our licensing guide for your state.

How much do roofing companies make?

A solo roofing contractor can earn $50,000–$100,000/year. A company with one crew typically generates $300,000–$800,000 in annual revenue with 20–35% net profit margins. Established companies with multiple crews routinely exceed $1 million in revenue. The key is maintaining margin discipline as you grow — don't sacrifice profit for volume.

What insurance do I need?

At minimum: general liability ($1M/$2M), workers' compensation (required once you have employees), and commercial auto. Total insurance costs typically run $10,000–$25,000/year. This is higher than most trades because roofing is classified as high-risk work. Read our contractor insurance cost guide for details.

How do I get my first roofing customers?

The fastest paths: storm chasing after hail/wind events (door-knock affected neighborhoods), offering free roof inspections, setting up a Google Business Profile and collecting reviews early, networking with real estate agents and property managers, and referral incentives. Most new roofing companies get their first 10 customers through some combination of door-knocking and personal network referrals.

What is the profit margin on a roofing job?

Healthy residential roofing margins range from 25–40%. On a $12,000 re-roof, materials might cost $3,500–$4,500, labor $2,500–$3,500, overhead $1,000–$1,500, leaving $2,500–$4,500 in profit. Commercial margins are typically tighter (15–25%) due to competitive bidding. Always calculate your actual costs before bidding — don't guess. See our contractor profit margin guide.

Should I start as a subcontractor first?

If you're new to running a business, yes. Working as a sub for 1–3 years under an established company lets you learn estimating, project management, insurance processes, and customer relations with less financial risk. You'll also build a reputation and contact network. Once you have experience and savings, transitioning to your own company is much smoother.

The Bottom Line

Roofing is a high-revenue, high-margin trade that rewards professionalism, safety, and consistency. The startup costs are higher than many trades, but the earning potential is significantly higher too. A single crew doing 3–4 jobs per week at healthy margins can generate $300,000–$500,000 in annual revenue.

The companies that win in roofing are the ones that do it right: proper licensing, full insurance, written contracts, safety-first culture, manufacturer certifications, and relentless follow-up on customer satisfaction. In an industry plagued by fly-by-night operators, being legitimate is your competitive advantage.

Start with the foundation — license, insurance, equipment — and build from there. Your first roof is out there waiting. Go find it.
