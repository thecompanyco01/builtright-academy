This guide gives you that blueprint, complete with real numbers from the plumbing industry.

Why Plumbers Need a Business Plan

The plumbing industry has a dirty secret: most owner-operators don't actually know their numbers. They charge what seems competitive, hope for enough calls, and scramble when a slow month hits. A business plan forces you to:

  • Know your real hourly cost — not just what you pay yourself, but vehicle, insurance, tools, marketing, and overhead
  • Set prices that actually generate profit — most plumbers underprice by 20–30%
  • Predict seasonal dips — so you can save during busy months to cover slow ones
  • Get financing — SBA loans, equipment financing, and credit lines all require a plan
  • Make better decisions — should you hire? Buy a second van? Add drain cleaning? Your plan gives you the data to decide.

Section 1: Executive Summary

One page. Cover these points:

  • Company name, location, legal structure (LLC recommended)
  • Services: Residential repair, new construction, commercial, drain cleaning, water heater install, etc.
  • Target market: "Homeowners within 30-mile radius of [city] with homes 15+ years old"
  • Revenue target: Year 1 realistic goal
  • Funding request: If seeking financing, state the amount and use
  • Competitive advantage: Same-day service? Flat-rate pricing? Specialty (gas line, tankless)? State it clearly.

Flat-Rate vs. Time & Materials

Our recommendation: flat-rate pricing. Here's why:

  • Customers know the price upfront — reduces "sticker shock" and disputes
  • Faster plumbers earn more (rewards efficiency, not slow work)
  • Easier to train new hires on pricing
  • Higher average tickets vs. T&M

Use a flat-rate price book. ServiceTitan and Housecall Pro have built-in ones, or build your own in a spreadsheet. Price each task at 1.5–2x your true cost (labor + materials + overhead).

Section 5: Marketing Plan

Top 5 Marketing Channels for Plumbers

  1. Google Business Profile (Free — highest ROI)

    90% of plumbing customers search "plumber near me." Your GBP listing is the first thing they see. Complete every field, get reviews aggressively, post weekly.

  2. Google Local Service Ads ($15–$75/lead)

    Pay-per-lead, not pay-per-click. You only pay when someone actually contacts you. Google-guaranteed badge builds trust.

  3. Nextdoor (Free to claim business)

    Hyperlocal recommendations from neighbors. Respond to help requests in your service area. One great review on Nextdoor can generate 5–10 calls.

  4. Referral Program ($50–$100 per referral)

    Give every customer a referral card. $50 off their next service for every new customer they send. Track referrals in your CRM.

  5. SEO Content (Free — long-term)

    Blog posts like "How Much Does a Water Heater Cost in [Your City]?" attract local search traffic for years. See our guide on plumber marketing strategies for detailed tactics.

Section 6: Operations & Growth

Daily Operations

  • 5:30 AM: Review today's schedule, load van with parts for booked jobs
  • 7:00 AM – 5:00 PM: Run service calls (target 4–6 jobs/day)
  • 5:00 – 6:00 PM: Respond to estimates, send invoices, follow up on unpaid
  • Evening: Review tomorrow's schedule, restock van as needed

When to Hire

  • You're turning down 5+ calls/week → time for tech #1
  • Revenue consistently above $18K/month → can afford a tech ($20–$25/hr)
  • Your time is better spent on sales/estimates than turning wrenches

Common Mistakes in Plumbing Business Plans

  1. Pricing based on competitors, not costs. Your competitor might be losing money. Price based on YOUR costs + YOUR target margin. Use our markup calculator.
  2. No marketing budget. Plan 8–12% of revenue for marketing in years 1–2. After that, 5–8% is fine if you have strong reviews and referrals.
  3. Ignoring seasonality. Plumbing is less seasonal than HVAC, but still has peaks (winter pipe freezes, spring remodels). Plan for 20% revenue variation.
  4. Trying to do everything. Don't offer commercial, new construction, AND residential from day one. Pick one, master it, then expand.
  5. No emergency fund. A broken-down van or a slow month can kill a new plumbing business. Keep 3 months of expenses in reserve.

FAQ

How much does it cost to start a plumbing business?

$41,000–$92,000 for a full setup. You can start leaner at $15,000–$20,000 if you already have a truck, tools, and your master plumber license. The biggest variable is whether you buy or finance a vehicle.

How much can a plumbing business owner make?

Solo owner-operators typically net $60,000–$120,000/year. Owners of plumbing companies with 3–5 trucks can net $150,000–$300,000+. The key variable is whether you transition from doing work to managing/selling work.

Do I need a master plumber license to start a business?

In most states, yes — you need a master plumber license to pull permits and operate independently. Some states allow a journeyman to work under a master plumber's license. Check your state's licensing board requirements.

What's the most profitable plumbing service?

Drain cleaning has the highest margins (65–75%) because it's low on materials and high on skill/equipment. Water heater installations have the best margin-to-ticket ratio. Repiping and sewer replacement offer the highest total dollar profit per job but are less frequent.
