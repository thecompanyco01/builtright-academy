How to Convert Watts to Amps: Complete Guide

Understanding the relationship between watts, amps, and volts is essential for any electrical work — from sizing breakers to choosing the right wire gauge. Here's everything you need to know.

The Basic Formulas

For single-phase AC circuits:

Amps = Watts ÷ (Volts × Power Factor)

Watts = Amps × Volts × Power Factor

For three-phase AC circuits:

Amps = Watts ÷ (Volts × √3 × Power Factor)

Watts = Amps × Volts × √3 × Power Factor

For DC circuits (no power factor):

Amps = Watts ÷ Volts

Understanding Power Factor

Power factor (PF) is the ratio of real power to apparent power. Resistive loads like heaters, incandescent lights, and electric ranges have a PF of 1.0. Inductive loads draw more current:

  • PF 1.0: Heaters, incandescent lights, toasters
  • PF 0.95: LED drivers, modern electronics
  • PF 0.85–0.90: Motors (HVAC compressors, pumps)
  • PF 0.80: Fluorescent lights, older motors
  • PF 0.60–0.70: Unloaded motors, cheap power supplies

Why it matters: A 1,000W motor with PF 0.85 at 120V actually draws 9.8 amps, not 8.3 amps. That extra current matters for wire sizing and breaker selection.

5 Electrical Mistakes That Trip Breakers

  1. Overloading a circuit. A 15-amp circuit maxes out at 1,440W continuous (80% rule). Two space heaters on one circuit = guaranteed trip.
  2. Ignoring power factor for motors. Motors draw more amps than their wattage suggests. Always use the nameplate amps, not calculated amps from watts.
  3. Using 14-gauge wire on a 20-amp breaker. This is a code violation and fire hazard. 20-amp circuits require 12-gauge minimum.
  4. Not accounting for voltage drop. Long wire runs (50+ feet) lose voltage. Use the next size up wire gauge for runs over 50 feet.
  5. Confusing 120V and 240V calculations. A 240V appliance draws half the amps of the same wattage at 120V. Always check the nameplate voltage.
