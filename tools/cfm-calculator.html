How to Calculate CFM: Complete Guide

CFM (cubic feet per minute) measures the volume of air moved. Whether you're sizing HVAC ducts, selecting an exhaust fan, or designing a ventilation system, getting the right CFM is critical for air quality, comfort, and energy efficiency.

The Basic CFM Formula

For room ventilation based on air changes per hour (ACH):

CFM = (Length × Width × Height × ACH) ÷ 60

ACH (air changes per hour) is how many times the entire volume of air in a room is replaced each hour. Different rooms need different ACH rates based on their use.

Exhaust Fan Sizing Rules

  • Bathrooms (≤100 sq ft): Minimum 50 CFM per IRC code. Prefer 1 CFM/sq ft.
  • Bathrooms (>100 sq ft): 1 CFM per square foot of floor area.
  • Kitchen range hoods: 100 CFM per linear foot of range for wall-mounted; 150 CFM/ft for island hoods.
  • Garage: 1 CFM per square foot, or 10 ACH, whichever is greater.
  • Attic fans: 1 CFM per square foot of attic floor area (minimum). Add 20% for dark roofs.

5 Common CFM Mistakes

  1. Undersizing exhaust fans. A cheap 50 CFM fan in a large master bath leads to mold. Size to 1 CFM per square foot minimum.
  2. Ignoring static pressure. Ductwork creates resistance. Long runs, elbows, and transitions reduce effective CFM. A fan rated at 100 CFM might only deliver 60 CFM through a long duct run.
  3. Not balancing supply and return. If your supply CFM exceeds return CFM, you create positive pressure that pushes conditioned air out through cracks. Returns should handle at least 80% of supply CFM.
  4. Using high-velocity ducts everywhere. Above 900 FPM, ducts get noisy. Keep bedroom and living area branches under 600 FPM.
  5. Forgetting makeup air. Large exhaust systems (kitchen hoods, shop ventilation) need makeup air to replace what's being exhausted. Without it, you get negative pressure, backdrafting, and doors that won't close.

Aspect ratio rule: Keep rectangular ducts at 4:1 aspect ratio or lower (width to height). Higher ratios waste material and increase friction. A 12×6 duct (2:1) is far more efficient than a 24×3 duct (8:1) even though both have similar area.
