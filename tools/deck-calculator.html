How to Estimate Deck Materials & Cost

Building a deck is one of the best ROI home improvements — averaging 65–75% return on investment at resale. But getting the material estimate wrong means budget overruns and extra trips to the lumber yard. Here's how to plan it right.

Joist Spacing Guide

Joists are the horizontal framing members that support the decking boards. Proper spacing is critical for preventing bounce, sag, and premature decking failure.

  • 16" on center — Standard for most decking materials run perpendicular to joists. Works for 5/4×6 PT pine, cedar, and most composites.
  • 12" on center — Required for diagonal decking patterns (45°), some thinner composite products, and decks that will hold heavy loads (hot tubs, large planters).
  • 24" on center — Only acceptable for 2×6 or thicker decking run perpendicular. Not recommended for composite.
  • Always use 2×8 or 2×10 joists for spans over 8 feet. 2×6 joists max out at about 8 feet between supports.

Permit Requirements

Most municipalities require a building permit for decks that are:

  • Over 30 inches above grade at any point (nearly universal requirement)
  • Attached to the house (ledger board attachment affects house structure)
  • Over 200 sq ft in some jurisdictions, even at ground level
  • Near property lines — setback requirements typically 5–10 feet from property lines

Permit costs: $75–$500 depending on jurisdiction and deck size. Plan 2–6 weeks for permit approval. Pro tip: call your local building department before you start — they'll tell you exactly what you need.

Pro Tips for Deck Building

  1. Crown joists up. Every board has a slight curve (crown). Install joists with the crown facing up so weight pushes them straight, not into a deeper bow.
  2. Use joist tape on the tops of all joists. It costs $30–$50 per deck and prevents moisture rot where decking screws penetrate — doubling joist life.
  3. Pre-drill hardwood decking. IPE and similar hardwoods will split if you drive screws without pilot holes. Use a combination drill/countersink bit.
  4. Leave 1/8" gaps between decking boards for drainage and expansion. Use a 16d nail as a spacer.
  5. Stagger board ends. Never have two adjacent boards ending on the same joist — it looks bad and concentrates stress.
  6. Use structural screws, not nails, for all framing connections. Simpson Strong-Tie SD screws or GRK structural screws are code-approved and much easier than through-bolts.
