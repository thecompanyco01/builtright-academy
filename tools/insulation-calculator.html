Understanding R-Value

R-value measures thermal resistance — how well insulation resists heat flow. Higher R-value = better insulation. The R-value you need depends on where you live (climate zone) and where you're insulating (attic, walls, floors).

Key fact: R-values are additive. Two layers of R-19 gives you R-38. This is useful for attic insulation where you can stack batts or add blown-in over existing insulation.

How Much Can You Save on Energy Bills?

Proper insulation is one of the highest-ROI home improvements:

  • Attic insulation: Saves 10–50% on heating/cooling. Pays for itself in 1–3 years.
  • Wall insulation: Saves 10–20%. Payback in 3–5 years.
  • Crawlspace insulation: Saves 10–20% and reduces moisture issues.
  • Air sealing + insulation: Combined savings of 15–40% on energy bills.

5 Insulation Mistakes That Waste Energy (and Money)

  1. Compressing insulation. Stuffing R-19 batts into a 3.5" wall cavity reduces the R-value to about R-13. Use the right thickness for your cavity.
  2. Leaving gaps and voids. Even small gaps dramatically reduce effectiveness. Insulation must fill the entire cavity with no air pockets.
  3. Ignoring air sealing. Insulation slows heat transfer but doesn't stop air leaks. Seal gaps around wires, pipes, and outlets before insulating.
  4. Blocking soffit vents in the attic. Attics need airflow. Use baffles to keep insulation away from soffit vents.
  5. No vapor barrier where needed. In cold climates, install a vapor barrier on the warm side (interior). In hot/humid climates, it goes on the exterior side.
