How to Calculate Rebar for Concrete Slabs

Rebar transforms a brittle concrete slab into a reinforced structure that can handle loads, temperature swings, and ground movement. Getting the right amount — size, spacing, and accessories — is critical. Here's everything you need to know.

Rebar Placement Rules

  1. Minimum concrete cover: 3 inches from the bottom of the slab to the rebar (for slabs on grade). This protects the steel from moisture and corrosion.
  2. Overlap (lap splice): When rebar isn't long enough to span the full slab, overlap pieces by at least 40 bar diameters. For #4 rebar: 40 × 0.5" = 20 inches minimum overlap.
  3. Edge setback: Rebar should stop 3 inches from the slab edge. Don't run it all the way to the form.
  4. Position in slab: For a 4" slab, rebar should sit at roughly 1.5–2 inches from the bottom — supported by chairs. For thicker slabs (6"+), you may need two layers (top and bottom mat).
  5. Tie every intersection: Use tie wire at every point where bars cross. Alternating is acceptable on large slabs but every intersection is better.

For any slab that carries vehicle traffic or structural loads, use rebar — not wire mesh. Mesh is fine for decorative patios and sidewalks.

Money-Saving Tips

  • Buy from a rebar supplier, not Home Depot. Big-box stores charge $8–$15 per 20-ft bar. Rebar yards charge $0.40–$0.70/lb — typically 30–50% less.
  • Order pre-cut and pre-bent rebar for footings and walls. It saves hours of cutting and bending on site. Most suppliers offer this at minimal extra cost.
  • Use #4 at 18" spacing for most residential work. It's the most cost-effective combination that meets code for standard slabs.
  • Don't skip the chairs. Rebar sitting on the ground is worse than no rebar — it creates a corrosion path. Chairs cost pennies and prevent thousands in future repairs.
  • Tie wire goes fast. Budget 2–3 lbs of tie wire per 100 intersections. Buy an extra roll — you'll use it.

Common Rebar Mistakes

  • No chairs: Rebar on the ground = no reinforcement. It must be elevated to the correct position within the slab.
  • Insufficient overlap: Bars that barely touch don't transfer load. Overlap must be 40× the bar diameter minimum.
  • Too close to edges: Rebar within 1–2 inches of the surface will corrode and crack the concrete. Maintain 3" cover.
  • Walking on rebar mat: Workers walking on the rebar grid push chairs out of position and bend bars. Place plywood walking paths during the pour.
  • Wrong size for the job: Using #3 rebar where #4 or #5 is needed will result in a structurally deficient slab. Follow engineering specs.
